Chapter 1: - Introduction to the topic

1.1 - Problem Domain

Global Positioning system (GPS) is able to accurately determine the outdoor location of objects or people by using signals from many satellites at the same time to calculate the distance of the object or people with a technique known as trilateration[1]. But it becomes inaccurate when trying to use it to determine the position of a person or an object within a building or any indoor location. The signals provided by the GPS satellites are easily degraded once there is no direct line-of-sight with the satellites from the object connecting to it. Also, GPS cannot determine the floor in a building a person or an object is. Since the information provided the GPS is not accurate, there is a need to find suitable approach that can provide significantly better result.

The need to determine the location or position of objects and people in a building has become more important in recent times. Businesses, for example, museums want to be able to locate the part of a building where a visitor is based on signals received from static sensory devices such as beacons and radio frequency tags. Other businesses such as shopping-malls, hotels, airports, universities, inventory and logistics companies want to be able to track and monitor movement in and out of their facilities, to provide personalized services to their customers and personal users want to automate control processes in the home using smart devices to turn on and off the lights, to change the ambience of a room, to play music and watch their favourite shows on a Smart TV and to identify any person within the house. IoT devices are already an integral part of a smart home and can be leveraged on to determine the proximity of objects or people within the room or the house whichever the case may be.[2].

1.2 - Introduction to Indoor User Location Identification in a smart home

First of all, it is ideal to understand what a smart home is and one good definition says that: “A smart home, or a smart house, is a home that incorporates advanced automation systems to provide the inhabitants with sophisticated monitoring and control over the building's functions. For example a smart home may control lighting, temperature, multimedia, security, window and door operations, as well as many other functions”[3].

Year by year, the sector of smart home buildings is increasing in the number of participants and this has led to a typical smart home having more capabilities and features. One of the new features is the voice controlling where a user using spoken words, is able to command virtual assistant agents(Google assistant or Cortana for example)by voice and all functions which are mentioned in core definition should be implemented by this virtual assistant agents.

1.3 - Our research direction

Voice command for the smart home is a new trend and smart home systems need to receive user commands via voice. At the same time, smart home systems should be aware of the location of the user while receiving these commands, in order to provide better services. For example, if a user walks to the kitchen at night and says “Switch the light on”, then the system should switch the light on up until the user reaches the Kitchen. The user does not need to mention the sepcific light, there might be multiple lights in and around the Kitchen. For example, on the corridors, the stairs. There have been many ways to know the approximate location of the user such as using Bluetooth Beacons, RFID tags or WiFi technologies. But all of those approaches require the user to carry a device.
Therefore, we have decided to base our research on the use of voice location for indoor user location identification in a smart home.

Chapter 2 - Most common positioning techniques

2.1 - Received signal strength indicator (RSSI)

The received signal strength is a very simple approach which is widely used for indoor location identification. It works on the principle that the closest receiver to the transmitting device will have the strongest signal. It can easily be used to provide proximity services for many technologies. It is the fundamental technique used in WiFi positioning system and bluetooth low energy devices. [6].

2.2 - Fingerprinting

Fingerprinting uses signal measurements across buildings to compute one’s position. Every transmitting device is pre-recorded with a unique identification. This makes it possible to determine the device from which a particular signal is coming from. Then, it becomes possible to compare received signals with existing records to figure out current position. However, fingerprinting does have a limitation. First, it only works when in motion, and second, it requires continuous stable signals over time. This technique can either be used alone or combined to improve the accuracy of other positioning technologies (usually those based on trilateration). Moreover, it is possible to simultaneously combine fingerprinting from multiple sources to increase accuracy. Here’s a list of technologies that can take advantage of fingerprinting: Radio frequency identification, Bluetooth low energy and WiFi.

2.3 - Trilateration

Trilateration positioning works in a similar way to how trilateration works with outdoor positioning where it relies on up to four satellites connecting to calculate the exact distance an object or people are. In order to apply it to indoor positioning, we use computed distances between several emitters and a receiver to compute the position of the latter. Distances are determined either by RSSI or by Time of Flight algorithms. ToF is a method that measures the distance between an emitter and a device and is based on the time difference between the emission of a signal and its return to the sender. Unlike RSSI, ToF involves roundtrip communication and complex signal processing that require dedicated chipsets. This comes with both a better accuracy and a higher cost. Here are technologies that can make use of trilateration: Bluetooth low energy, WiFi, Visible lights, etc.

Chapter 3 - Technologies for indoor positioning with their advantages

3.1 - Wireless Local Area Network (WLAN) or (WIFI)

It is a technology based on the IEEE 802.11 standard. It is used to provide wireless network connection between different devices and to connect to the Internet in mixed environments. WiFi has become ubiquitous since it is readily available in almost all smart phones, laptops other portable devices. This makes WiFi an ideal candidate for positioning system and one of the most widely researched positioning technologies. Since there are existing wireless access points, we can easily connect any WiFi-enabled devices to it. WiFi can utilize received signal strength indicator (RSSI) technique to determine the proximity of an object or person based on the level of the signal received. It is also one of the cheapest technologies that can be used to implement an indoor positioning system since all the required devices are readily available [7].

3.2 - Bluetooth low energy (BLE)

Bluetooth positioning systems makes use of Bluetooth technology to implement location identification. Radio-based devices operate on the principle of receiver and transmitter. Bluetooth low energy (BLE) devices popularly known as beacons are used to transmit the emitted signals that can be read either by smartphones located in the emission area. The more deployed beacons, the more accurate the position. User position is then associated to the location of the BLE device with the strongest signal. Different positioning techniques can be used in BLE, but the most widely implemented technique is the RSSI based systems. It is very similar to how a WiFi-based positioning works. It can also be combined with other techniques to increase the accuracy. [8].

Advantages Disadvantages
Cost-effective, simple hardware such as beacons Additional hardware
Low energy consumption App is required for client-based solutions
Flexible integration into the existing infrastructure (battery-powered or power supply via lamps and the domestic electrical system) Relatively small range (up to 30m)
Works where other positioning techniques do not have a signal
Compatible with iOS and Android
High accuracy compared to WiFi (up to 1m)

3.3 - Ultra-Wideband (UWB)

Ultra-wideband is undoubtedly the most precise method of indoor positioning. It involves placing UWB anchors around the corners of a space that is up to 25m apart and also attaching a UWB locator tag to an asset to be tracked[9]. The UWB tag sends radio signal pulses between 3 - 7GHz which can be used by the anchors to determine the position of the tag with up to 30cm accuracy.
Nevertheless, this technology has several drawbacks: lack of standardization that results in no smartphone available and high cost.

Advantages Disadvantages
Immune to interference Shorter range
Provides high accuracy Requires extra hardware on different user devices
High cost

3.4 - Radio frequency identification (RFID)

RFID is a form of wireless communication that uses radio waves to identify objects or people. RFID-based positioning techniques can be split into two main categories: tag-oriented and reader-oriented. The former is used to locate RFID tags while the latter is to find the position of portable RFID readers. [10]. The data transmitted by RFID tags are read using a predefined RF and protocol. There are two basic types of RFID systems.
Active RFID: Active RFID tags continue to transmit signals in the Ultra High Frequency (UHF) and microwave frequency range and passive RFID: can operate without any battery but have lesser accuracy compared with the active ones

Advantages Disadvantages
Consumes low power, has wide range Localization accuracy is low

3.5 - Visible light communication (VLC)

VLC make use of light emitting diodes (LED) to transmit light signals which can be read by light sensors to determine the positioning and the direction of the LED emitters. Angle of Arrival technique is the most used to implement a VLC for positioning [11]. The advantage of visible light communication is that it can easily be installed in a house in a large scale without disrupting anything. The downside is that it requires a direct line of sight communication for it to work.

Advantages Disadvantages
Lamps are extensively and homogeneously available in buildings Comparatively higher power consumption
Beacons can complement positioning Range is affected by obstacles
Modern LED are energy efficient Primarily requires Line of Sight
VLC is precise (less than 1 meter) and has a high range (up to 8 meters)
No disturbing, eye-catching, costly hardware
Works cross-platform
Wide-scale availability
Potential to provide high accuracy
Multipathfree

3.6 - Acoustic signal

Sound-based identification technology can take advantage of microphone sensors which are ever present in mobile devices to emit sounds from a sound source which can be used to provide proximity. It can make use of time of arrival technique or angle of arrival to provide an estimate of the distance from the sound source to its destination. [12].
In order to effectively use this method, it is recommended to make use of grid-like set of speakers like the Matrix Voice. These are capable devices which can easily pick up sound from all directions of the room. Using an angle of arrival or direction of arrival technique, we can determine the direction of travel from the sound source to the receiver, this helps to provide the angle the sound emanates from.

4.3 - Discovering capabilities of Matrix Voice

It is obvious that we need microphones which can take clear voice from long distance even if the voice level is really low. In order to know the reliability of clear voice delivery from Matrix Voice, we have decided to integrate Matrix Voice with Google Assistant and test the distance by speaking from different distances. In other words, we have customized Google Assistant which uses Matrix Voice for voice input and we have spoken to that Customized Google Assistant from different range of distance such as starting 1 meter up to 15 meters and from collected data, we could conclude that the voice between 1 and 12 meters is fully delivered and from 12 meters the voice’s quality got low and Google Assistant was not recording the correct context.

4.4 - Determining the direction of the voice

The direction of the user’s voice can be determined with a Matrix Voice due to the fact that Matrix Voice has microphones which are placed in certain cardinalities and from those cardinalities can determine the direction of the voice. As Figure 4.2, shows that each microphone has X and Y cardinalities and each microphone produces distinct voice strength, depending on which microphone is targeted.

As long as the voice is recorded, each microphone produces different voice strength as it is shown in Figure 4.2. For example, as in Figure 4.3, the user speaks towards the microphones and we can check the voice strength and compare it with actual voice strength of each microphone. In the Figure 4.4, we have described that a user is speaking to Microphone M3 and it has voice strengths between 12 000 and 16 000, and we can compare the taken voice strength in that range as it is shown. If the received voice strength is between those range, we can say that a user is speaking to Microphone M3 and most importantly from this point, we can determine the direction due to cardinalities of M3.
Having understood the working principles of obtaining the direction of the voice, we can conclude that this approach can be enhanced by increasing the number of Matrix Voice devices (input points). To get an optimal direction of the voice, it is enough to have 3 Matrix Voice input points. The Figure 4.5 shows that we can obtain an intersection point of all cardinalities of 3 Matrix Voice while those 3 Matrix Voice will receive the voice and produce different cardinalities for a targeted microphone.
